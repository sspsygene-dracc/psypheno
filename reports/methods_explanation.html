0. Overview & Pipeline

Each gene in the SSPsyGene database can appear in multiple datasets and, within a single dataset, can have multiple associated p-values (e.g., one per perturbation experiment that affected it). Our goal is to combine these p-values into a single summary statistic per gene that captures total evidence across all experiments.

The pipeline for each gene proceeds as follows:

  1. Collect raw p-values from every dataset table that declares a pvalue_column. A single gene may contribute multiple p-values per table (e.g., it was a target of several perturbations) and across many tables.
  2. Pre-collapse (for Fisher/Stouffer only): reduce each table's p-values for that gene down to a single per-table p-value using min(p) × n, capped at 1.0.
  3. Combine using four methods: Fisher and Stouffer operate on the collapsed per-table p-values; CCT and HMP operate directly on all raw p-values.

Fisher and Stouffer require at least 2 collapsed table p-values (both < 1.0) to produce a result; otherwise they return NULL. CCT and HMP can operate on any number of p-values ≥ 1.

1. Pre-Collapse: Bonferroni Within-Table Correction

Problem: A gene may appear in multiple rows of the same data table. For instance, in a perturbation screen, gene G might be a differentially expressed target in experiments where 5 different risk genes were knocked down. That gives us 5 p-values for G from a single table. These p-values are not independent—they all come from the same assay measuring the same gene—and we should not feed them individually into Fisher or Stouffer as though they were independent studies.

Solution: For each gene-table combination, we compute a single representative p-value:

ptable = min( min(p1, …, pn) × n,   1.0 )

where n is the number of rows (p-values) for that gene in that table, and p1, …, pn are the raw p-values from those rows.

Rationale
This is the Bonferroni correction applied to the minimum. The classical Bonferroni bound says that if we test n hypotheses, then padjusted = min(n · pmin, 1) controls the family-wise error rate. Here we are treating the multiple rows as multiple “looks” at the same gene within one table: we take the best (smallest) p-value but penalize it by the number of looks. This is conservative (it over-corrects when p-values are positively correlated, which they typically are) but it is simple and guarantees that we do not inflate significance from within-table multiplicity.
Implementation
collapsed_pvals = []
for _tbl, tbl_pvals in table_dict.items():
    n = len(tbl_pvals)
    collapsed = min(min(tbl_pvals) * n, 1.0)
    collapsed_pvals.append(collapsed)
Who uses it?
Fisher's method and Stouffer's method. Both assume that the inputs are independent p-values, one per “study” (here, one per table). The CCT and HMP, being robust to correlation, operate on the full set of raw p-values directly and skip this step.

2. Fisher's Method

Fisher's method (Fisher, 1932) is the oldest and most widely used p-value combination technique. The idea is elegant: under the null hypothesis, each p-value is Uniform(0, 1), so −2 ln(p) is distributed as χ2(2). Sums of independent chi-squared variables are themselves chi-squared.

Test statistic
X2 = −2 ∑i=1k ln(pi)
where k is the number of tables (after pre-collapse) and pi is the collapsed p-value for table i.
Null distribution

Under H0 (all individual null hypotheses are true) and with independent p-values:

X2 ~ χ2(2k)

The combined p-value is then P(χ2(2k) ≥ X2), i.e., the survival function of the chi-squared distribution with 2k degrees of freedom.

Why it works — step by step
  1. If p ~ Uniform(0, 1), then −ln(p) ~ Exponential(1).
  2. An Exponential(1) variable is the same as Gamma(1, 1), and 2×Exponential(1) = χ2(2).
  3. Therefore −2 ln(p) ~ χ2(2) for each p.
  4. Sums of independent χ2 variables: χ2(d1) + χ2(d2) = χ2(d1+d2).
  5. Hence X2 = ∑ −2 ln(pi) ~ χ2(2k).
Independence assumption
Step 4 above requires that the χ2(2) terms are independent, which requires the p-values to be independent. When p-values are positively correlated, Fisher's method tends to be anti-conservative (too many false positives). This is why we use the pre-collapse step: it reduces the inputs to one per table, and different tables (different datasets/experiments) are plausibly independent.
Implementation
_, fisher_p = combine_pvalues(valid_collapsed, method="fisher")

scipy.stats.combine_pvalues computes exactly the formula above. We filter out collapsed values of exactly 1.0 before combining (they contribute ln(1) = 0 to the statistic, i.e., no information) and require at least 2 remaining values.

Fisher, R.A. (1932). Statistical Methods for Research Workers, 4th ed.

3. Stouffer's Method

Stouffer's method (Stouffer et al., 1949) is similar in spirit to Fisher's but works through the normal distribution rather than the chi-squared. Each p-value is converted to a Z-score via the inverse normal CDF, and the Z-scores are averaged (or summed and normalized).

Test statistic
Z = ∑i=1k Φ−1(1 − pi) √k

where Φ−1 is the quantile function (inverse CDF) of the standard normal distribution, and k is the number of (pre-collapsed) p-values.

How the transform works
  1. If p ~ Uniform(0, 1) under H0, then Φ−1(1 − p) ~ Normal(0, 1). (This is just the probability integral transform run in reverse.)
  2. Each p-value is thus converted to a standard normal Z-score. A small p-value yields a large positive Z; a large p-value yields a Z near zero or negative.
  3. The sum of k independent standard normals is Normal(0, k), so dividing by √k gives Normal(0, 1).
Null distribution
Z ~ Normal(0, 1)  under H0

The combined p-value is P(Zobs ≥ Z) = 1 − Φ(Z).

Independence assumption
Like Fisher's method, the derivation relies on the Z-scores being independent (step 3). With correlated inputs, the variance of the sum is no longer k, and the method breaks down. We again rely on the pre-collapse to ensure approximate independence of the combined inputs (one per table).
Comparison with Fisher
  • Fisher's method is more sensitive to one very small p-value (it drives the log-sum).
  • Stouffer's method responds more evenly to moderate signals across many studies.
  • With equal weights, both are widely used and often give similar results.
Implementation
_, stouffer_p = combine_pvalues(valid_collapsed, method="stouffer")

Same pre-filtering of 1.0 values and minimum count of 2 as Fisher's method.

Stouffer, S.A. et al. (1949). The American Soldier, Vol. 1.

4. Cauchy Combination Test (CCT)

The CCT (Liu & Xie, 2020) was designed specifically for settings where the input p-values may be correlated. It exploits a special property of the Cauchy distribution to achieve robustness to arbitrary dependency structures.

Test statistic
T = ∑i=1L wi · tan( (0.5 − pi) · π )

where L is the total number of raw p-values and wi = 1/L (equal weights, summing to 1).

The key transform: Uniform → Cauchy

This is the mathematical heart of the method. Consider a single p-value p ~ Uniform(0, 1) under H0:

  1. Start: p ~ Uniform(0, 1).
  2. Shift and scale: 0.5 − p ~ Uniform(−0.5, 0.5).
  3. Multiply by π: (0.5 − p) · π ~ Uniform(−π/2, π/2).
  4. Apply tangent: tan((0.5 − p) · π) ~ Cauchy(0, 1).

Step 4 is a classical result: if U ~ Uniform(−π/2, π/2), then tan(U) follows a standard Cauchy distribution. This can be verified by computing the CDF: P(tan(U) ≤ x) = P(U ≤ arctan(x)) = (arctan(x) + π/2) / π, which is exactly the Cauchy CDF.

Why the Cauchy distribution is special

The Cauchy distribution has a remarkable property: any weighted sum of independent Cauchy random variables is again Cauchy. Specifically:

If Ci ~ Cauchy(0, 1) independently, then ∑ wi Ci ~ Cauchy(0, ∑ |wi|)

With our weights wi = 1/L, we have ∑ wi = 1, so T ~ Cauchy(0, 1) under independence.

Robustness to dependency — the key insight

Here is why the CCT is special compared to Fisher or Stouffer. Under dependency:

  • For Fisher's method, the sum of log-transformed p-values no longer follows χ2(2k); the variance changes and the null distribution is wrong.
  • For Stouffer's method, correlated Z-scores inflate the variance of the sum beyond k.
  • For the CCT, even under dependency, the Cauchy distribution has such heavy tails that the tail behavior of the sum of dependent Cauchy variables is dominated by the maximum term. Liu & Xie (2020) proved that for the significant tail (i.e., when the combined statistic is large), the distribution of T is well-approximated by Cauchy(0, 1) regardless of the dependency structure.

More precisely, their Theorem 1 shows that for any joint distribution of the p-values whose marginals are Uniform(0, 1):

P(T > t) P(C > t) → 1   as t → ∞

where C ~ Cauchy(0, 1). This means that in the region that matters for hypothesis testing (the far right tail, where T is large and the combined p-value is small), using Cauchy(0, 1) as the reference distribution is asymptotically correct. The heavy tails of the Cauchy “absorb” the effect of correlation.

Combined p-value
pcombined = P(Cauchy(0, 1) > T) = 1 2 − arctan(T) π

This is just the survival function (SF) of the standard Cauchy distribution.

Implementation
def _cauchy_combination(pvalues):
    weights = np.ones(len(pvalues)) / len(pvalues)
    p_clamped = np.clip(pvalues, 1e-300, 1.0 - 1e-15)
    t_stat = np.sum(weights * np.tan((0.5 - p_clamped) * np.pi))
    combined_p = cauchy_dist.sf(t_stat)
    return float(np.clip(combined_p, 0.0, 1.0))

Note: p-values are clamped to avoid numerical issues — p = 0 would send tan to −∞ and p = 1 to +∞.

No pre-collapse
Because the CCT is robust to dependency, we feed it all raw p-values directly. Multiple correlated p-values from the same table are handled gracefully by the method's theoretical guarantees.
Liu, Y. & Xie, J. (2020). Cauchy combination test: a powerful test with analytic p-value calculation under arbitrary dependency structures. Journal of the American Statistical Association, 115(529), 393–402.

5. Harmonic Mean P-Value (HMP)

The HMP (Wilson, 2019) is another dependency-robust method that uses a different mathematical route: the harmonic mean. It was developed specifically for combining p-values from genome-wide studies where correlation structures are complex and unknown.

Definition

With weights wi = 1/L for each of the L p-values:

HMP = ∑i wi ∑i wi / pi = 1 ∑i (1/L) / pi = L ∑i 1 / pi

This is simply the harmonic mean of the p-values. The harmonic mean is always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean, and is strongly influenced by small values — exactly the behavior we want for combining p-values.

Intuition

Consider what 1/p represents: it is a measure of “evidence strength.” A p-value of 0.001 gives 1/p = 1000; a p-value of 0.5 gives 1/p = 2. The HMP computes the harmonic mean, which is dominated by the largest 1/p values (the smallest p-values), giving a combined measure that is driven by the strongest evidence.

Null distribution and the Landau distribution

Under H0, if pi ~ Uniform(0, 1) and the weights are equal and sum to 1, Wilson (2019) proved two key results:

  1. Exact distribution: For finite L, the distribution of 1/HMP under H0 follows a Landau distribution (a heavy-tailed, positively-skewed distribution that arises in physics for energy loss of charged particles). The Landau distribution is a stable distribution with characteristic exponent α = 1. This means 1/HMP has a particularly heavy right tail.
  2. Asymptotic validity (Theorem 1): When the weights are normalized (i.e., ∑ wi = 1, which is our case with wi = 1/L), the HMP is asymptotically a valid p-value for small values. That is:
    P(HMP ≤ u) ≤ u + o(1)   as u → 0
    This holds under arbitrary dependency among the p-values.
The Landau-bound adjustment — and why we do not use it

In the original paper, Wilson also described a conservative multiplicative correction for finite samples: multiply HMP by L to obtain a corrected p-value. This correction arises from bounding the tail probability of the Landau distribution. For unnormalized weights (or when exactness is needed at non-asymptotic p-values), this factor of L provides a guaranteed upper bound on the type I error.

However, Theorem 1 of Wilson (2019) shows that with normalized weights (our case), the HMP itself is already an asymptotically valid p-value for small values — no multiplicative adjustment is needed. The Landau tail bound becomes unnecessary because the normalization ensures the correct scaling.

Summary: With wi = 1/L, ∑ wi = 1, and the HMP can be used directly as a combined p-value. The “Landau bound” correction (multiply by L) is for unnormalized weights or when conservative finite-sample guarantees are needed. Our implementation skips it, relying on the asymptotic result.
Robustness to dependency

The theoretical guarantee of Theorem 1 holds under arbitrary dependency. The proof works by showing that 1/HMP = ∑ wi / pi, and each 1/pi has a Pareto(1) distribution (for p ~ Uniform(0,1), 1/p − 1 ~ Pareto(1)). Sums of Pareto(1) variables (which have infinite mean) converge to a stable law whose tail behavior is controlled regardless of the dependency structure. This is analogous to why the CCT works: heavy-tailed distributions are “forgiving” of dependence.

Implementation
def _harmonic_mean_pvalue(pvalues):
    n = len(pvalues)
    weights = np.ones(n) / n  # w_i = 1/L, sum = 1
    hmp = np.sum(weights) / np.sum(weights / pvalues)
    return min(float(hmp), 1.0)

Since ∑ wi = 1, the numerator is just 1, and the formula simplifies to L / ∑(1/pi) = harmonic mean.

No pre-collapse
Like the CCT, the HMP operates on all raw p-values directly, relying on its theoretical robustness to handle within-table correlations.
Wilson, D.J. (2019). The harmonic mean p-value for combining dependent tests. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 116(4), 1195–1200.

7. Why All Four Methods?

We compute all four combination methods because they have complementary strengths and the “true” dependency structure among our p-values is unknown:

Method Input Dependency Sensitivity
Fisher Collapsed (per-table) Requires independence Driven by strongest single signal
Stouffer Collapsed (per-table) Requires independence Responds evenly to moderate signals
CCT All raw p-values Robust to arbitrary dependency Tail-driven (heavy-tail property)
HMP All raw p-values Robust to arbitrary dependency Driven by small p-values (harmonic mean)

Key trade-offs:

  • Fisher and Stouffer are canonical, well-understood, and have been the default choice in meta-analysis for decades. By using pre-collapsed per-table p-values, we approximate independence. However, even after collapsing, subtle dependencies may exist (e.g., overlapping cell lines or shared experimental conditions across datasets).
  • CCT and HMP are newer methods specifically designed for settings with unknown or complex dependency structures. They use all raw p-values (more data, more power in some settings) and do not require the pre-collapse step. The trade-off is that they are asymptotically valid (accurate for small combined p-values) rather than exactly valid at all significance levels.
  • In practice, all four methods tend to produce similar gene rankings, especially at the top (most significant genes). When they diverge, examining which method ranks a gene differently can provide insight: for instance, a gene that is significant under Fisher but not HMP may be driven by a single very small p-value from one table, whereas HMP is more robust to such isolated signals.

By reporting all four, we provide users with a richer picture and allow them to apply the interpretation that matches their assumptions about the data.
